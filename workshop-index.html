Quantum currency workshop · step 5 · direction · second cube

The wave has been observed.
Now it chooses a direction.

In quantum mechanics, observation collapses the wave function — the superposition of all possible states resolves into one. The second cube is that moment: potential held in the first cube now commits to a direction. But the quantum approach differs from classical planning in one essential way: before the direction is chosen, all three futures are modelled simultaneously. The choice comes after the seeing, not before.

"Thorny ground cannot commit to one vector — every direction gets crowded out. The second cube asks: what does this system actually choose?"

The three futures are not optimistic, realistic, and pessimistic. They are contraction, steady-state, and expansion — each a coherent system in its own right, each with its own financial logic. The direction chosen is the vector that holds through all three: the decision that makes sense whether the system contracts, holds, or grows.

Three futures · one direction

Future 1

Contraction

If revenue dropped by 30% tomorrow — what holds? What gets released? What becomes clearer?

Future 2

Steady state

If nothing changes — what is the honest trajectory? Where does this system arrive in 12 months?

Future 3

Expansion

If the unchosen potential from Step 4 were activated — what becomes possible? What does the torus look like when fully coherent?

What direction holds through contraction, steady state, and expansion simultaneously?

That direction is the vector. Everything in Steps 6–9 is built from it.

Why three futures simultaneously

Classical financial planning models one future — usually the one that feels most likely, or the one that feels most aspirational. Both are distortions. The quantum approach models three simultaneously because the system will encounter all three at different points — and the decisions made now need to hold across all of them. A direction chosen only for the expansion future creates fragility. A direction chosen only for contraction creates unnecessary constraint. The vector that holds through all three is the one with structural integrity.

Contraction as clarification

The contraction future is not a catastrophe scenario. It is a clarification tool. When you model what holds if revenue drops by 30%, you discover what is actually essential to the system — what the torus cannot release without ceasing to function. This is the irreducible from Step 3 made financially concrete. Teams that have done the contraction modelling honestly find that it removes anxiety rather than generating it — because the unknown is more frightening than the named.

Direction signals — what to listen for

Direction found

A choice emerges from the scenario modelling that feels obvious once it's named — not because it's easy, but because it's coherent. The room relaxes around it. There's a quality of "of course" rather than "I suppose." The vector holds.

Direction crowded out

Every scenario generates a different preferred direction. The team keeps qualifying — "it depends," "if X then Y, but if not then Z." This is thorny ground at the direction step. The discernment from Step 3 may not have gone deep enough. Return to the irreducible before proceeding.

Direction bypassed

The expansion future absorbs all the energy and the contraction future is skipped or minimised. The team is choosing a direction from the best-case scenario only. This produces a plan, not a vector — and plans without vectors don't survive contact with contraction.

The vector — what it is and isn't

The vector is not a goal. It is not a revenue target. It is not a milestone. The vector is the direction the system has chosen to move — the orientation that governs decisions across all three futures. "We are moving toward financial self-sufficiency" is a vector. "We are moving toward a model where our time is priced at its actual value" is a vector. "We are moving toward a team structure where the founder is not the single point of failure" is a vector. Each of these holds in contraction, steady state, and expansion — not identically, but directionally.

What this step tends to produce

The most common revelation in Step 5 is that the direction the system needs to move has been known for some time — and the scenario modelling simply makes it undeniable. The contraction future names what can't be sustained. The steady-state future names where the current trajectory leads. The expansion future names what the system is actually reaching for. The vector that holds through all three is almost always the thing that has been circling at the edge of the canvas since Step 4. The second cube doesn't create the direction. It makes it visible.

Model all three futures before choosing a direction. Do not skip contraction. The scenario that feels most uncomfortable to model is usually the one that contains the most important information. The direction chosen after honest contraction modelling is structurally sound. The direction chosen without it is a wish.
